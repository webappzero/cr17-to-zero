What is this talk about?

This talk focuses a spotlight on the science of learning. Just how much do we value learning itself as a subject worthy of careful study? And what are we prepared to do about it?

What aren't the goals of this talk?

-Not a comprehensive tutorial on the science of learning! -Not a comprehensive introduction to Clojure.

Who is the target audience?

  • Existing Clojure programmers
  • Aspiring programmers

Both of these groups include scientists, entrepreneurs, people that want to help solve the world's problems. It's hard to think of an ambitious project in any of these areas whose success won't be directly dependent on its use of software technology.

I myself want to start a software company. My decade long experience as an IT professional taught me that I will either run my software company, or it will run me. That's why I've chosen Clojure and why I'm so enthusiastic about sharing it with programming newcomers.

What are the goals of this talk?

  • For programmers, presumably Clojure programmers: to pique your interest in the science of learning by examining its history, tenets and potential benefits.
  • For aspiring programmers: To attract and welcome you to the Clojure community. To make sure that, even if you consider yourself a mere mortal, average like me, that you have the confidence to get started today, knowing that you can achieve your learning goals, and that you will have an amazing and supportive community in Clojure, to guide you along the way.

And besides all that, it's a great excuse to talk about two of my favorite things: Clojure & Learning!
